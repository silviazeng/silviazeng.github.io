When my parents visit Los Angeles — and then an aunt, an uncle, the whole older generation arriving in waves — the Getty Center is always on the itinerary. It's free, it's spectacular, and it perches on a hill above the city with its own little tram to carry you up. The one weak link was the guide.

The Getty's own guides aren't built for them. The audio guide is a scavenger hunt of three-digit numbers; the wall text assumes you read English easily and already know why a Pontormo is worth stopping for. In Chinese there's essentially nothing. And even the English material has the museum's built-in problem — it tells you about *everything*, which is a polite way of telling you about nothing. Hand someone in their seventies the museum's entire collection and a cheerful "enjoy," and you've scheduled them an afternoon of sore feet and quiet disappointment.

So I built my own. It comes in two halves.

The first is a **read guide** (读·指南): about thirty works actually worth crossing a gallery for, each with a proper write-up — who made it, why it matters, exactly which room it hangs in, and, because I can't help myself, *what the Getty paid to get it*. It opens with a suggested route through the campus — West, then North, then South — so nobody doubles back on tired feet.

The second is a **walk map** (走·地图): every pavilion, floor by floor, hand-redrawn to match the Getty's official gallery plans, with a numbered pin dropped on each must-see. Tap a pin for the short version.

A couple of things I'm quietly proud of. The map knows where you are: a small "where am I" marker wakes up when your phone is actually on the Getty's hill. And it remembers where you've been — tap through the rooms and it lays a trail of footprints behind you, so you can see what you've covered and what's still ahead. Everything is set in large type, because it was made for eyes older than mine.
